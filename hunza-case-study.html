01 — Overview

Two premium brands. One website. A lot of users to please.

Hunza Lighting and LuxR — two distinct premium outdoor lighting brands — needed to be merged onto a single, unified website without losing the identity of either. The existing Hunza site was functional but dated, and the LuxR presence was minimal. Neither reflected the quality of the products they sold.

I led the full UX/UI redesign: from auditing the existing site and mapping user behaviour, through wireframing and high-fidelity design, to building and launching the final product in WordPress with custom HTML, CSS, JS and PHP.

02 — The Problem

A premium brand let down by its own website.

01

Confusing navigation

Users — architects, homeowners, distributors — couldn't quickly find what they needed. Product categories were unclear, and the path from landing to enquiry involved too many steps.

02

No defined user journeys

The site treated everyone the same. Architects specifying commercial projects have completely different needs to a homeowner browsing garden lighting — neither was being served well.

03

Visual hierarchy mismatch

Hunza products are premium, precision-engineered, and beautifully made. The website didn't communicate any of that. The design was actively undercutting the brand at every point.

04

Two brands, no unified home

LuxR and Hunza needed to coexist without one cannibalising the other — maintaining separate product identities while presenting a single, coherent company to the world.

07 — Reflection

What I took away from this.

"Merging two brands isn't just a design challenge — it's a negotiation between identities, audiences, and stakeholders. Getting it right meant listening before designing."

This project taught me that the hardest design problems aren't visual — they're structural. Before a single screen was designed, I needed to understand who was using the site, what they actually needed, and how two distinct brands could share space without conflict.

Working directly with distributors and sales reps gave me feedback that changed decisions I thought were settled. The gap between what stakeholders assume users want and what users actually do is always bigger than expected.

Building and maintaining the site myself meant every design decision had to be practical to implement, and I refined constantly based on what worked in production. That feedback loop made me a much sharper designer.
