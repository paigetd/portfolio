01 Overview

Two brands, one site, one person to fix everything.

🌏

Hunza has been manufacturing premium outdoor lighting for over 30 years. It's a multinational company with distributors covering the entire world — from New Zealand and Australia to Europe, North America, and the Middle East. The website wasn't just a brochure. It was the digital face of a global brand with real revenue and reputation on the line.

🙋
I was the entire marketing and digital team.

No agency. No design lead. No developer to hand off to. Every decision, every line of code, every illustration, every spec sheet, every social post — researched, designed, built, and shipped by me. This was not a collaborative project with a team. It was a one-person overhaul of a live premium brand's entire digital and print presence.

🚀
Ready for launch — validated by real users

The redesign is complete and ready to go live. It hasn't launched yet, but it's been through multiple rounds of refinement based on direct feedback from the people who will actually use it — including international distributors who flew in to see it in person.

Hunza Lighting and LuxR are two distinct premium outdoor lighting brands that needed to be merged onto a single website without losing the identity of either. I was brought on as Marketing Coordinator and ended up leading the full digital overhaul — from UX strategy through to building and maintaining the site in WordPress with custom HTML, CSS, JS, and PHP.

This wasn't a greenfield project. The existing Hunza site had real structural problems, a broken product database, and three different user audiences all being served the same generic experience. Everything had to work properly, not just look good.

Mid-project I also uncovered and fixed a database-level pricing bug via SQL — products showing incorrect prices on a site where individual fixtures cost thousands of dollars is not a minor issue. I found it, diagnosed it, and fixed it myself.

02 Discovery + Research

Before designing anything, I had to understand everyone.

I started with a site audit and direct conversations with the sales team and distributors — the people closest to the customers. Three distinct user types emerged, each with fundamentally different goals and mental models for what a lighting brand's website should do.

📐

Persona 01

The Architect

"I need technical data fast. Give me IP ratings, beam angles, IES files, and installation specs — then get out of my way."

Blocked by: Specs buried in generic product tabs. No CAD downloads on product pages. Can't compare multiple fixtures quickly.

🏡

Persona 02

The Homeowner

"I want to see what this looks like installed. Help me imagine it in my garden and tell me how to find a local installer."

Blocked by: No inspirational photography. No project gallery. Distributor finder buried or non-existent.

📦

Persona 03

The Distributor

"I'm ordering in bulk. I need trade login access, current pricing, and spec sheets I can send to my customers immediately."

Blocked by: No trade portal that worked. Generic contact form instead of a dedicated trade enquiry path. Resources page impossible to scan.

🗣

Stakeholder interviews

Spoke directly with the Hunza sales team and key distributors to understand what customers were asking for and where they were falling off.

  • Architects leaving before finding spec data
  • Distributors emailing for documents that should be self-serve
  • LuxR generating brand confusion post-acquisition
🔍

Site audit

Audited every page of the existing site for information architecture, broken functionality, and UX failures before writing a single design brief.

  • Database pricing errors across a subset of products
  • Navigation hierarchy serving no audience clearly
  • No mobile-optimised product pages

09 Technical Challenge

The problem design alone couldn't fix — and that I found and fixed alone.

Midway through the project I discovered a significant number of products displaying incorrect prices. For a brand where individual fixtures can cost several thousand dollars, this wasn't a minor bug — it was a trust and revenue issue.

No one else had noticed. I found it during a routine product audit, investigated the database via phpMyAdmin, identified duplicate and conflicting SKU entries causing the pricing logic to pull from wrong records, and wrote a targeted SQL query to identify and correct all affected SKUs.

I then added validation logic to prevent the conflict recurring — so the fix wasn't just a patch, it was a safeguard.

Pricing corrected site-wide
Zero recurrence post-fix
Found and fixed solo — no dev support
fix_duplicate_skus.sql
-- Find all duplicate SKUs
SELECT post_id,
       meta_key,
       meta_value
FROM wp_postmeta
WHERE meta_key = '_sku'
GROUP BY meta_value
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

-- Validate fix post-cleanup
SELECT COUNT(*) as dupes
FROM wp_postmeta
WHERE meta_key = '_sku'
GROUP BY meta_value
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

11 Testing + Iteration

What changed after feedback.

Feedback — distributors

Product filtering wasn't granular enough

Trade distributors couldn't filter by the specifications they actually spec to: IP rating, beam angle, wattage. They were manually cross-referencing external documents.

What I changed

Added technical spec filtering to product listings

Extended the filter system to include technical attributes. Distributors could now filter by IP rating, wattage, and application type directly from the listing page.

Feedback — sales team

LuxR felt like an afterthought

Sales reps reported customers asking whether LuxR still existed. The merger had made it feel absorbed rather than featured alongside Hunza.

What I changed

Gave LuxR dedicated landing pages

Created brand-specific section pages for LuxR within the unified structure, retaining shared navigation while giving LuxR its own visual identity and product pages.

Feedback — distributors (in person)

Trade portal needed to actually work

The old trade portal existed but pricing was wrong so no one used it. Distributors needed a portal they could trust — with accurate pricing and easy access to spec sheets.

What I changed

Built a functional trade portal with correct data

Fixed the underlying pricing data (via SQL), redesigned the portal interface, and surfaced technical resources prominently. Validated with distributors during factory visits.

Constraints

  • No dedicated dev resource — design and build were both my sole responsibility at all times
  • Existing WooCommerce data couldn't be fully restructured without breaking live integrations
  • Brand guidelines for both Hunza and LuxR were loose, requiring interpretation rather than strict application
  • The site had to remain live and fully functional throughout the entire redesign — no opportunity for a clean slate

13 Reflection

What I took away.

Merging two brands isn't just a design challenge. It's a negotiation between identities, audiences, and business reality. Getting it right meant listening before designing.

This project taught me that the hardest problems aren't visual, they're structural. Before a single screen was designed, I needed to understand who was using the site, what they needed, and how two distinct brands could share space without one feeling secondary. That took research, not assumptions.

Working directly with distributors and sales reps — especially presenting to international distributors when they visited the factory — changed design decisions I thought were settled. The gap between what stakeholders assume users want and what users actually need is always larger than expected.

The SQL fix was also a reminder that a designer who can move across the full stack is a different kind of asset. Problems don't stay in their lanes. The database bug wasn't in my job description — it was just blocking the product from working, and I was the only one there to fix it.

Presenting to a room of international distributors who've been selling Hunza products for decades and hearing unanimous positive feedback — that's validation you can't get from a portfolio review. It confirmed I'd solved real problems for real people.
