Hunza & LuxR.

01 — Overview

Two brands, one site, and a lot of problems to fix.

Hunza Lighting and LuxR are two distinct premium outdoor lighting brands that needed to be merged onto a single website without losing the identity of either. I was brought on as Marketing Coordinator and ended up leading the full digital overhaul — from UX strategy through to building and maintaining the site in WordPress with custom HTML, CSS, JS, and PHP.

This wasn't a greenfield project. The existing Hunza site had real structural problems, a broken product database, and three different user audiences who were all being served the same generic experience. Everything had to work properly, not just look good.

02 — The Problem

A premium brand let down by its own website.

Before starting design work, I audited the existing site thoroughly. The visual design issues were obvious. But digging deeper revealed structural problems that no amount of good design could paper over. The site was actively losing enquiries because users couldn't find what they needed, and the product database had pricing errors caused by SKU conflicts in the backend.

01

No audience distinction

Architects specifying commercial projects, homeowners browsing, and trade distributors all landed on the same page with no differentiated path forward.

02

Visual design undercut the brand

Hunza products are precision-engineered and expensive. The website looked cheap. Every page was actively damaging trust with potential customers.

03

Broken product database

SKU conflicts in the database were causing products to display incorrect pricing — a serious problem for a brand where products cost thousands of dollars.

04

Two brands, no unified home

LuxR existed as a separate site with minimal presence. Neither brand was benefiting from the relationship, and users had no way to discover both.

03 — User Flow

Core flow: from landing to enquiry.

The original site had one path for everyone. The redesign introduced audience-based entry points — the user self-selects their context and is routed to content relevant to them.

Homepage
Select audience
Browse products by application
View specs + tech docs
Enquire or find distributor

Previously: Homepage → Products (undifferentiated) → Contact (if they could find it). Average 6 clicks to enquiry. Redesigned to 3.

05 — Technical Challenge

The problem design alone couldn't fix.

Midway through the project I discovered that a significant number of products were displaying incorrect prices on the site. For a brand where individual fixtures can cost several thousand dollars, this wasn't a minor bug — it was a trust and revenue issue.

After investigating the WordPress database through phpMyAdmin, I identified that duplicate and conflicting SKU entries were causing the pricing logic to pull from the wrong product records. I wrote a targeted SQL query to identify all affected SKUs, then systematically corrected the database entries and added validation to prevent the conflict recurring.

Technical implementation

Problem: Duplicate SKU entries in the WooCommerce product database were causing incorrect prices to display across a subset of products. Affected items were showing prices from unrelated variants.

Diagnosis: Used phpMyAdmin to query the wp_postmeta table and identify duplicate _sku meta keys. Cross-referenced with wp_posts to map the conflicts.

SELECT post_id, meta_key, meta_value
FROM wp_postmeta
WHERE meta_key = '_sku'
GROUP BY meta_value
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

Fix: Cleaned duplicate entries, standardised SKU format across all product records, and updated the pricing logic to enforce SKU uniqueness at the application layer. All prices validated correct post-fix.

07 — Testing & Iteration

What changed after feedback.

Feedback from distributors

Product filtering wasn't granular enough

Trade distributors found they couldn't filter products by the specifications they actually spec to (IP rating, beam angle, wattage). They were having to manually cross-reference external documents.

What I changed

Added technical spec filtering to product listings

Extended the product filter system to include technical attributes. Distributors could now filter by IP rating, wattage, and application type directly from the listing page. Feedback after launch was positive.

Feedback from sales team

LuxR felt like an afterthought on the merged site

Sales reps reported that customers were asking whether LuxR still existed as a brand — the merger had made it feel like it was being absorbed rather than featured.

What I changed

Gave LuxR dedicated landing pages and brand expression

Created brand-specific section pages for LuxR within the unified site structure — retaining the shared navigation while giving LuxR its own visual identity and product pages. Customer confusion reduced significantly.

Constraints I worked within

  • No dedicated development resource — design and build were both my responsibility, so scope had to be realistic
  • Existing WooCommerce product data couldn't be fully restructured without breaking live integrations, which shaped the database fix approach
  • Brand guidelines for both Hunza and LuxR were loose, requiring interpretation rather than strict application
  • The site had to remain live and functional throughout the redesign — no clean-slate opportunity
08 — Outcomes

What it actually delivered.

Unified brand, stronger presence

Hunza and LuxR on one platform reduced confusion and improved perceived credibility. Sales reps reported fewer "can you explain how the brands are related" queries from customers post-launch.

Reduced friction to enquiry

Audience-based navigation cut the path from landing to enquiry from 6 clicks to 3. Distributor feedback confirmed the trade section was significantly easier to navigate for specifying products.

Pricing errors resolved across all products

The SQL fix corrected pricing on all affected products. No further pricing discrepancies were reported in the months following the fix, removing a significant trust and revenue risk.

09 — Reflection

What I took away.

"Merging two brands isn't just a design challenge. It's a negotiation between identities, audiences, and business reality. Getting it right meant listening before designing."

This project taught me that the hardest problems aren't visual, they're structural. Before a single screen was designed, I needed to understand who was using the site, what they needed, and how two distinct brands could share space without one feeling secondary. The SQL work in particular showed me that being a well-rounded designer means not stopping at the boundary between design and engineering.

Working directly with distributors and sales reps changed design decisions I thought were settled. The gap between what stakeholders assume users want and what users actually need is always larger than expected. Building and maintaining the site myself meant decisions had to be practical as well as considered — and that constant feedback loop made me a sharper designer.
