Splashy: Make an Impact.

My Role

UX Research · UI Design · Illustration

Platform

iOS Mobile App (Figma)

Audience

Children aged 8 to 12

Illustration

All artwork hand-drawn in Illustrator

01 Overview

Getting kids to care about the planet, without lecturing them.

Splashy is a gamified iOS app that turns eco-friendly habits into something a child actually wants to do. Kids complete real-world quests, earn sea creatures as rewards, and watch their personal ocean transform from polluted to thriving.

The core insight driving this project came from research: traditional environmental education increases knowledge but doesn't change attitudes (Rice et al., 2024). Kids know plastic is bad. What they lack is a tangible connection between their actions and the outcome. Splashy closes that gap.

I owned the full process: user research with primary school children, competitive analysis of educational apps, persona development, wireframing, three major design iterations, original illustration in Adobe Illustrator, and a fully interactive Figma prototype.

02 Research Foundation

Evidence that shaped the direction.

📊

Plastic production

From 2 million tonnes in 1950 to over 450 million tonnes in 2019. 1-2 million tonnes enter oceans annually. (Ritchie, Samborska & Roser, 2023)

📚

Knowledge ≠ Attitude

Traditional conservation education increased knowledge but didn't change attitudes. Emotional connection matters. (Rice et al., 2024)

🎮

Gamification works

Gamification increases learning outcomes, especially for primary students in offline-adjacent environments. (Li, Ma & Shi, 2023)

Competitive Analysis

Earth Hero

Tracks environmental impact with carbon calculators and goal setting. But data is presented as graphs and charts — unengaging for children. Not targeted at kids.

Takeaway: Visualisation needs to be immediate and emotional, not abstract.

Duolingo ABC & Khan Academy Kids

Simple UI, colourful mascots, voice-over guidance, and tactile feedback. Designed for children who may not be strong readers yet.

Takeaway: Interface should work without reading. Icons and characters do the heavy lifting.

05 Logo Development

From rough concept to final mark.

The logo needed to feel friendly, signal environmental themes, and work at app-icon scale. This took several rounds of iteration.

Early Explorations

Initial sketches exploring fish concepts. The fish-in-a-bag felt too negative. The heart-shaped sprout fin (concept 3) became the foundation.

Refined vector drafts — experimenting with outline weight and composition.

Final refinements — incorporating the recycling arrow motif and earth element.

What I learned: I initially wanted the same no-outline minimalism as the rest of the app, but found that thick outlines created a much more inviting and friendly feel — essential for a children's app. The final logo combines the fish, leaf fin, and recycling arrow to communicate "saving the earth" without words.

06 Wireframing

Structuring before styling.

I created paper prototypes and low-fidelity wireframes to test navigation logic and information hierarchy before investing in visuals.

Paper prototypes and early wireframes testing layout, navigation, and quest flow.

Key feedback from wireframe testing: The reward for levelling up and the reward for decreasing plastic usage being the same didn't make sense. I separated them — plastic reduction cleans the ocean; levelling up unlocks new fish. Bottom navigation icons felt boring — I explored more creative navigation solutions.

07 Asset Creation

Building the visual world.

All illustrations were hand-drawn in Adobe Illustrator. The fish characters and ocean environment set the visual tone for the entire app.

Initial fish character designs and environmental assets. Each fish species was designed to feel collectible.

I researched ocean environments and fish designs extensively — including watching Finding Nemo to study species variety, colour palettes, and underwater landscapes. The art style combines flat design minimalism with the depth and colour of games like Hungry Shark World.

09 Early Designs

The versions that taught me what not to do.

Before arriving at the final design, I built several iterations that failed in specific, instructive ways.

Home Screen — Early Version

What was wrong: Too many competing elements. The ocean was small, the mascot felt disconnected, and there was no clear primary action. Kids didn't know where to look first.

What I learned: Children need a single focal point. The ocean had to be the star.

Problem: Too many decisions — cognitive load too high.

Problem: Visuals too abstract — kids couldn't connect to real objects.

Problem: Input mechanism fiddly — felt like data entry, not play.

10 Iteration

Learning from what broke.

Each failed version pointed toward the same solution: simplify, make the ocean the hero, and ensure every action has immediate visual payoff.

Revised: Single clear CTA, mascot as welcoming presence.

Revised: Visual profile — avatar, ocean naming, progress tracking.

Revised: Impact visualised — ocean transforms, creatures grow.

11 Final Design

The version that worked.

After three major iterations, the final design incorporated everything I'd learned. The ocean is the hero, quests are icon-led and scannable, and every interaction feels like progress, not work.

Quests screen — final. Icon-led cards, one clear primary action per quest, visual progress indicators that don't require reading.

Quest detail view. When kids want more information, it's there — but the screen works even if they never read a word.

12 Testing + Iteration

What didn't work. What I changed. Why.

Problem found in testing

Quest screen had too much text

Children skipped reading entirely and tapped whatever button was biggest. The layout assumed a reading level the target age group doesn't have.

How I fixed it

Icon-led cards with one primary action

Reduced copy by ~60%, moved critical information into iconography, made the progress bar the dominant visual element.

Problem found in testing

The ocean didn't feel different enough

Children couldn't distinguish between dirty and slightly-less-dirty ocean states. The reward loop broke down.

How I fixed it

Distinct milestone states

Defined three clearly differentiated ocean states tied to creature unlocks. Kids immediately noticed and reacted.

Problem — wireframe feedback

Rewards were confusing

Levelling up and reducing plastic gave the same reward. Users didn't understand what drove what.

How I fixed it

Separated reward systems

Plastic reduction cleans the ocean. Levelling up unlocks new fish. Cosmetic items from quests. Clear, distinct incentives.

13 Outcomes

What the testing showed.

Reward system grasped without instruction

All test participants understood the quest-to-ocean connection without explanation. The visual feedback loop was legible enough that onboarding copy became unnecessary.

Collection mechanic created unprompted motivation

Kids asked "what's the next creature I can get?" without being prompted — a clear signal that the mechanic had taken hold.

Simplified UI cut task completion time

After the quest screen redesign, children completed the log progress action in under 10 seconds consistently.

14 Reflection

What this taught me.

Designing for children is humbling. Every assumption about how users think gets stripped away the moment you actually talk to them.

The biggest lesson was that research is not a phase you complete before design — it is structural to every outcome. The creature collection mechanic came from a child mentioning Pokemon. The named ocean came from a kid saying they'd want to show their friends. Those were not features I invented. They were features I heard.

I also learned how fundamentally different it is to design for a non-adult audience. Simplicity is not dumbing things down. It is doing the hard design work so the user does not have to. Touch target dimensions, reward timing, information density — every decision has to be reexamined from scratch when your user is 8 years old.

The process of moving from academic proposal through multiple milestones to final prototype taught me that iteration based on feedback is where the real work happens. My first drafts focused too much on aesthetics. Mentor feedback pushed me to think about interaction first — breaking each component down and understanding how users would actually use it before combining them.
