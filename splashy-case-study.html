01 — The vibe

Getting kids to care about the planet, without lecturing them.

For my university capstone, I designed Splashy, a gamified mobile app that encourages children to adopt eco-friendly habits in their everyday lives. Kids complete real-world quests, earn ocean creatures as rewards, and watch their personal ocean transform from polluted to thriving.

The project ran for one semester and required me to conduct original user research, define the product concept, design and iterate on the UI, and produce a fully interactive Figma prototype. Every character, creature, and illustration was hand-drawn in Adobe Illustrator.

02 — The struggle

Kids want to help. They just don't know how, or why it matters.

Sustainability education for children is largely adult-led and abstract. Posters in classrooms, statistics that mean nothing to a 7-year-old. The gap isn't interest — most kids genuinely care about animals and nature. The gap is agency and immediacy.

Existing apps were either too educational and boring, too complex for the age group, or didn't connect digital behaviour to real-world action. None made the child feel like their choices were building something that belonged to them.

03 — Listening to kids

Talking to the actual users.

I conducted semi-structured interviews with six children aged 7 to 11. Rather than asking what features they wanted (kids struggle with abstract hypotheticals), I asked about things they already loved: games they played, things they collected, what made them feel proud.

The sessions revealed three consistent patterns that directly shaped the product.

"I want to help but I don't know what to do."
→ Concrete, achievable quests tied to real actions.
"I love collecting things. Like Pokémon."
→ Creature collection mechanic, unlocking as you complete tasks.
"I'd want to show my friends my ocean."
→ Personalised, named ocean that evolves with real-world behaviour.

07 — Oops, we tested it

What didn't work, and what I changed.

I tested early prototypes with three of the original research participants. Kids don't sugarcoat feedback.

🚨 Problem
Quest screen had too much text
Children skipped reading and just tapped randomly. They didn't understand what to do.
✨ Fix
Icon‑led cards + one clear action
Reduced text by 60%, made the progress bar huge. Kids understood in seconds.
🚨 Problem
Ocean didn't feel different enough early on
Kids couldn't tell the dirty ocean from the slightly cleaner one. No reward feeling.
✨ Fix
Three distinct milestone states + creature unlocks
Polluted → Recovering → Thriving. Each milestone unlocks a new creature. Progress became tangible.
💭 Real talk – constraints I worked with
  • One semester timeline → no extended testing cycles
  • No parental dashboard (noted for future)
  • Quest content manually curated – a scalable system would be needed for real launch
  • Long‑term engagement features (streaks, social) deprioritised for MVP
08 — What actually happened

Results that made me smile.

Immediate understanding
All three test kids got the quest‑to‑ocean connection without any explanation. No instructions needed.
"What's the next creature?"
Kids asked unprompted. The collection mechanic was the main driver of engagement.
Task completion in under 10 seconds
After the redesign, children logged progress in less than 10 seconds, compared to confusion and mis‑taps in v1.
09 — What I learned
“Designing for children is humbling. Every assumption you have about how users think gets stripped away the moment you actually talk to them.”

The biggest lesson: research isn't optional, it's structural. I started with assumptions. The interviews showed me something completely different. The collection mechanic came from a child mentioning Pokémon. The named ocean came from a kid saying they'd want to show their friends. The best design decisions weren't mine — they came from listening.

Designing for a non‑adult audience means rethinking everything: typography, touch targets, reward timing, information density. Simplicity isn't dumbing down, it's doing the hard work so the user doesn't have to.

If I continued this project, next steps would be a parental dashboard, scalable quest generation, and longitudinal testing to see if habits stick.
