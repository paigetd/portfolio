01 — Overview

Getting kids to care about the planet — without lecturing them.

For my university capstone, I designed Splashy — a gamified mobile app that encourages children to adopt environmentally friendly habits in their everyday lives.

The core mechanic: kids complete real-world quests (bringing a reusable bag to the supermarket, recycling correctly, reducing plastic use) and earn creatures and Ecopoints as rewards. These rewards populate a personal ocean — which starts brown and polluted, and gradually clears and fills with life as the child builds better habits.

Rather than preaching, Splashy makes the connection between behaviour and environmental impact visible, immediate, and theirs.

Every character, creature, illustration, and graphic in the app — the fish, the crab, the coral, the ocean scenes — was hand-drawn by me in Adobe Illustrator.

03 — The Problem

Kids want to help. They just don't know how — or why it matters.

Sustainability education for children is often abstract and adult-led — posters in classrooms, lectures from parents, statistics that mean nothing to a 7-year-old. The gap isn't interest; most kids genuinely care about animals and nature. The gap is agency.

Existing apps were either too educational (boring), too complex, or didn't connect digital actions to real-world behaviour. None made the child feel like their choices were making something beautiful happen.

01

Abstract impact

Children couldn't see the connection between their actions and environmental outcomes.

02

No agency

Existing tools talked at kids, not with them. No sense of ownership or personal achievement.

03

Short attention spans

Habit-building apps for children need immediate, tangible rewards — not delayed gratification.

04

No personalisation

Generic content doesn't resonate. Kids needed something that felt like theirs.

04 — Research

Talking to the actual users — the kids.

I conducted interviews with children in my target age group to understand how they currently think about the environment, what motivates them, and what kinds of rewards they'd actually care about. This shaped every design decision that followed.

"I want to help but I don't know what to do."

Kids had the desire but no clear pathway to action. This became the foundation for the quest system — concrete, achievable tasks.

"I love collecting things. Like Pokémon."

The collection mechanic (unlocking ocean creatures) came directly from this. Kids are motivated by accumulation and completion.

"I'd want to show my friends my ocean."

The personalised ocean — named after the user — taps into pride and identity. Your ocean is uniquely yours.

05 — Core Mechanic

The ocean that grows with you.

The central design decision was making environmental impact visible and personal. Every quest completed clears the water a little more. Every reward unlocked adds a creature. The ocean becomes a living record of the child's real-world behaviour.

07 — Reflection

What this taught me.

"Designing for children is humbling. Every assumption you have about how users think gets stripped away the moment you actually talk to them."

This project gave me my first real taste of research-led design — and it fundamentally changed how I approach problems. I started with assumptions about what kids needed. The interviews showed me something different.

The pivot to a collection mechanic came directly from a child telling me they loved Pokémon. The named ocean came from a kid saying they'd want to show their friends. The best design decisions in this project weren't mine — they came from listening.

I also learned how different it is to design for a non-adult audience: everything needs to be simpler, more immediate, more delightful, and more forgiving. Typography, iconography, touch targets, reward timing — all of it needs rethinking from the ground up.
