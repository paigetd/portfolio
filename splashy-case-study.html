Case Study — UX Research + Mobile App Design

Splashy:
Make an
Impact.

A gamified iOS app that turns eco-friendly habits into an ocean you can watch come alive.

Role

UX Research + UI Design + Illustration

Platform

iOS Mobile App (Figma)

Audience

Children aged 6 to 12

Year

2023 — University Capstone

01 — Overview

Getting kids to actually care about the planet.

Splashy is a gamified iOS app that gets children aged 6 to 12 to build real eco-friendly habits. Kids complete real-world quests, earn sea creatures as rewards, and watch their personal ocean transform from polluted to thriving.

The core design challenge: how do you create a meaningful loop between a child sorting recycling in their kitchen and a creature appearing in a digital ocean? Every design decision came back to that gap. Make it too long, too complex, or too invisible, and the product fails.

I owned the full process from research to prototype: semi-structured interviews, concept definition, information architecture, UI design across three major iterations, and a fully interactive Figma prototype. Every character and sea creature was hand-drawn in Adobe Illustrator.

02 — The Problem

Kids want to help. They just don't know how — or feel like they can.

Sustainability education for children is largely adult-led and abstract. The gap is not interest — most kids genuinely care about animals and the planet. The gap is agency and immediacy. Nothing on the market closes it in a way that respects how children actually think.

01

Abstract impact

Children could not draw a line between their daily actions and environmental outcomes. For a 7-year-old, invisible consequences do not stick.

02

No sense of ownership

Existing tools talked at children rather than with them. Nothing felt personalised. Without ownership, sustained engagement does not happen.

03

Rewards came too late

Kids operate on short feedback loops. Long-term reward systems built for adults fail at this age entirely. Motivation evaporates before any payoff arrives.

04

No real-world link

Apps that lived entirely on-screen missed the point. Behaviour change happens in the physical world, at a tap, in a kitchen, at school.

03 — Research

Talking to the actual users.

I conducted semi-structured interviews with six children aged 7 to 11. Rather than asking what features they wanted, I asked about things they already loved: games they played, things they collected, moments that made them feel proud.


The goal was to understand motivation, not preferences. Three consistent patterns emerged across all six participants and directly determined every major product decision. None of these ideas came from me. They came from listening carefully and taking children seriously as research participants.

"I want to help but I don't know what to do."

Kids had the desire but no pathway. This became the foundation for the quest system: concrete, named, achievable real-world tasks rather than vague environmental messaging.

"I love collecting things. Like Pokemon."

The creature collection mechanic came directly from this. Kids are intrinsically motivated by accumulation and completing a set. Each eco-action unlocks a new creature as an immediate reward.

"I'd want to show my friends my ocean."

The personalised named ocean taps into pride and identity. Your ocean is uniquely yours, shaped by your choices over time, and something worth showing off.

04 — User Flow

The loop had to be ruthlessly short.

If there were more than three taps between completing a task and seeing a reward, kids would disengage. The entire interaction model was designed around minimising that gap.

Sign up + questionnaire
→
View assigned quests
→
Complete real-world task
→
Log progress in app
→
Earn creature + Ecopoints
→
Ocean updates
Design constraint: real-world action to visible reward in 3 taps or fewer. This was tested against at every iteration.

07 — Testing + Iteration

What didn't work. What I changed. Why.

I tested early prototypes with three of the original research participants. Kids are the most direct usability testers you will ever work with. They do not soften their reactions.

Problem found in testing

Quest screen had too much text

Children skipped reading the instructions entirely and just tapped whatever button was biggest. The original layout assumed a reading level and attention span that the target age group does not have. This was not a reading problem. It was a design problem.

How I fixed it

Icon-led cards with one primary action

Reduced copy by roughly 60%, moved critical information into iconography, made the progress bar the dominant visual element. After the redesign, all three participants understood the screen without reading anything. Task completion time dropped significantly.

Problem found in testing

The ocean did not feel different enough early on

Children could not distinguish between the dirty and slightly-less-dirty ocean states. The transition was too gradual. Without a clear before/after moment, completing a quest felt meaningless and the reward loop broke down entirely.

How I fixed it

Distinct milestone states tied to creature unlocks

Defined three clearly differentiated ocean states with dramatically different visual palettes. Each state transition is tied to a creature unlock, creating a deliberate reward event rather than an imperceptible gradient shift. Kids immediately noticed and reacted to the change.

Constraints + Scope Decisions

  • University timeline meant one semester from research to prototype, with no time for extended testing cycles or multiple participant recruitment rounds
  • Parental dashboard was intentionally out of scope but flagged as a critical requirement for any real-world version — no responsible children's app ships without it
  • Quest content was manually curated for MVP scope — a production version would need a scalable editorial or generation system
  • Long-term engagement mechanics like daily streaks and social features were deprioritised to keep focus on proving the core loop
08 — Outcomes

What the testing showed.

Reward system grasped without instruction

All three test participants understood the quest-to-ocean connection without any explanation. The visual feedback loop was legible enough that onboarding copy became unnecessary — a strong signal the design was working at the right level.

Collection mechanic created unprompted motivation

Kids asked "what's the next creature I can get?" without being prompted — one of the clearest signals in usability testing that a mechanic has genuinely taken hold and is driving intrinsic motivation.

Simplified UI cut task completion time dramatically

After the quest screen redesign, children completed the log progress action in under 10 seconds consistently, versus repeated hesitation and wrong taps in v1. Removing text in favour of iconography solved the usability problem that text had created.

09 — If I Continued

What a v2 would need.

The MVP proved the core loop works. A real product would need three things before it could responsibly launch to children.

01

Parental dashboard

A parallel app layer for parents to review quest progress, approve completions, and manage content. Non-negotiable for a children's product — COPPA compliance and parental controls need to exist from day one.

02

Scalable quest system

Manual curation works for a prototype. A live product needs a content management system with difficulty scaling and category filtering. Seasonal quests tied to real environmental events would add meaningful depth.

03

Longitudinal testing

The biggest open question: do habits stick beyond the first session? A 4 to 6 week diary study with families is the minimum needed to measure whether Splashy changes real-world behaviour or just generates in-app engagement.

10 — Reflection

What this taught me.

Designing for children is humbling. Every assumption you have about how users think gets stripped away the moment you actually talk to them.

The biggest lesson from this project was that research is not a phase you complete before design. It is structural to every outcome. I came in with assumptions about what children needed from a sustainability app. The interviews showed me something completely different. The creature collection mechanic came from a child mentioning Pokemon unprompted. The named personalised ocean came from a kid saying they'd want to show their friends. Those were not features I invented. They were features I heard.

I also learned how fundamentally different it is to design for a non-adult audience. Simplicity in this context is not dumbing things down. It is doing the hard design work so the user does not have to. Typography sizing, touch target dimensions, reward timing, information density, reading level — every single decision has to be reexamined from scratch when your user is 8 years old. Nothing transfers from adult UX patterns without scrutiny.

The constraints were real and shaped the project honestly. A one-semester timeline means you can prove the concept or prove the details, rarely both. Splashy proved the concept: the loop works, the mechanic creates genuine motivation, and the visual system communicates without words. What comes next is harder and more important work.
