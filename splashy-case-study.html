01 Overview

Getting kids to care about the planet, without lecturing them.

Splashy is a gamified iOS app that turns eco-friendly habits into something a child actually wants to do. Kids complete real-world quests, earn sea creatures as rewards, and watch their personal ocean transform from polluted to thriving.

The design challenge was making the feedback loop short enough to matter to a 9-year-old. Every decision came back to one question: how many taps between doing something good in the real world and seeing it on screen?

I owned the full process: semi-structured user research, concept definition, information architecture, UI design through three major iterations, and a fully interactive Figma prototype. Every character and sea creature was hand-drawn in Adobe Illustrator.

02 Problem + Research

Evidence that shaped the direction.

Sustainability education for children is largely adult-led and abstract. The gap is not interest — the gap is agency and immediacy. Research confirmed this before any design work started.

Initial problem identification — mapping where existing tools fail children

Synthesising research findings into core design problems

01

Abstract impact

Children could not connect daily actions to environmental outcomes. Invisible consequences don't stick.

02

No ownership

Existing tools talked at children rather than with them. Without ownership, engagement doesn't happen.

03

Rewards too late

Kids operate on short feedback loops. Long-term reward systems built for adults fail completely at this age.

04

No real-world link

Apps that lived entirely on-screen missed the point. Behaviour change happens in the physical world.

03 Research

Who we're designing for.

Semi-structured interviews with six children aged 7 to 11. Rather than asking what features they wanted, I asked about things they already loved: games they played, things they collected, moments that made them feel proud.

Three consistent patterns emerged across all six. None of these ideas came from me. They came from listening carefully.

"I want to help but I don't know what to do."

Kids had the desire but no pathway. This became the foundation for the quest system: concrete, named, achievable real-world tasks.

"I love collecting things. Like Pokemon."

The creature collection mechanic came directly from this. Each eco-action unlocks a new creature as an immediate, tangible reward.

"I'd want to show my friends my ocean."

The personalised named ocean taps into pride and identity — uniquely yours, shaped by your choices over time.

08 Testing + Iteration

What didn't work. What I changed. Why.

Tested early prototypes with three of the original research participants. Kids are the most direct usability testers you will ever work with.

Problem found in testing

Quest screen had too much text

Children skipped reading instructions entirely and tapped whatever button was biggest. Not a reading problem — a design problem.

How I fixed it

Icon-led cards with one primary action

Reduced copy by roughly 60%, moved critical information into iconography. After the redesign, all three participants understood without reading anything.

Problem found in testing

The ocean didn't feel different enough early on

Children could not distinguish between dirty and slightly-less-dirty states. Without a clear moment, completing a quest felt meaningless.

How I fixed it

Distinct milestone states tied to creature unlocks

Three clearly differentiated ocean states with dramatically different visual palettes. Each tied to a creature unlock — a deliberate reward moment.

Constraints

  • University timeline — one semester from research to prototype
  • Parental dashboard out of scope but flagged as critical for any real-world version
  • Quest content manually curated — a production version needs a scalable system
  • Long-term engagement mechanics deprioritised to prove the core loop first
09 Outcomes

What the testing showed.

Reward system grasped without instruction

All three test participants understood the quest-to-ocean connection without any explanation — the visual feedback loop was legible enough that onboarding copy became unnecessary.

Collection mechanic created unprompted motivation

Kids asked "what's the next creature?" without being prompted — one of the clearest signals in usability testing that a mechanic has genuinely taken hold.

Simplified UI cut task completion time dramatically

After the quest screen redesign, children completed the log progress action in under 10 seconds consistently, versus repeated hesitation and wrong taps in v1.

10 Reflection

What this taught me.

Designing for children is humbling. Every assumption about how users think gets stripped away the moment you actually talk to them.

The biggest lesson was that research is not a phase you complete before design — it is structural to every outcome. The creature collection mechanic came from a child mentioning Pokemon unprompted. The named ocean came from a kid saying they'd want to show their friends. Those were not features I invented. They were features I heard.

Simplicity is not dumbing things down. It is doing the hard design work so the user does not have to. Touch target dimensions, reward timing, information density — every decision has to be reexamined when your user is 8 years old. Nothing transfers from adult UX without scrutiny.
